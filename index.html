My Path to Games

The thing you have to understand about me is that I do a lot of things.

I've had a long career in software development (mostly on the web), but I started out in graphic design. Somewhere along the way I built an encyclopedic knowledge of psychology. All the while I've been painting. I was part of an improv comedy troupe that had two shows a week downtown. I made some money writing a bunch of books, both fiction and nonfiction.

But games is what I've always come back to, they are my forbidden love. My stomach does flipflops of joy when I'm making games, but any game project I work on immediately takes over my brain. I dream about it. I'm thinking about the timing for the hoisting mechanic so much that I forget to eat sometimes. Forget trying to carry on a Real Job™ while doing a game on the side.

Creating games is so addictive to me, that I refused to allow myself to do it. "Why not work in the industry?" That question has been on my mind for two decades.

 

I told myself games were too trivial; there are real problems in the world, and I want to help solve those. Plus I was terrified of the notoriously bad working conditions.

Three things changed:

  1. I realized that games could be more. The technology that drives games could be used to build a better world.
  2. I realized that smaller studios were not sweatshops, and any overtime I pulled would be for love.
  3. I just *can't not* do it anymore. The pang of sadness every time I thought of developing games became just too much to bear.

My mind is clear now, the time is long past due, games here I come.
